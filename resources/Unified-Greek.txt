What are the three accents used in Ancient Greek?	Acute (´), Grave (`), and Circumflex (ˆ)			
What does the acute accent (´) indicate?	A rising or high pitch on the accented syllable (ὀξεῖα, &quot;sharp&quot;)			
What does the grave accent (`) indicate?	A lowered or neutral pitch — replaces the acute on the last syllable when another word follows			
What does the circumflex accent (ˆ) indicate?	A rise and fall in pitch on a single long syllable (περισπωμένη, &quot;bent around&quot;)			
Where can the accent fall in a Greek word?	Only on one of the last three syllables: ultima, penult, or antepenult			
Which accent can appear only on long vowels or diphthongs?	Circumflex (ˆ)			
When does the grave accent replace the acute?	When a word ending in an acute is followed immediately by another word (e.g.  ἀγαθός ἐστιν → ἀγαθὸς ἐστιν)			
Why can’t the circumflex appear on a short syllable?	Because it requires a long vowel or diphthong to allow both rise and fall of pitch			
Give an example of a circumflex accent	δῶρον — rise–fall tone on dô			
What does the accent system in Ancient Greek indicate: pitch or stress?	Pitch — the syllable is pronounced with a change in musical tone, not louder stress			
How do modern pronunciations treat accents?	As stress marks — simply place vocal emphasis on the accented syllable			
Mnemonic for remembering accent functions	“Acute rises, Circumflex bends, Grave falls.”			
Why do accent patterns matter in inflected forms?	They preserve word identity and help distinguish otherwise similar endings.			
What is the meaning of a macron (¯) in Ancient Greek?	It marks a long vowel sound (ā, ē, ī, ō, ū) — the vowel is held roughly twice as long as a short one.			
Which vowels can be either short or long in Ancient Greek?	α (alpha), ι (iota), and υ (upsilon)			
Which vowels are always short?	ε (epsilon) and ο (omicron)			
Which vowels are always long?	η (eta) and ω (omega)			
Which diacritic indicates shortness?	The breve (˘), e.g. ᾰ, ῐ, ῠ
